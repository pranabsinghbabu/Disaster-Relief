DO'S AND DONTS ON AVALANCE

Do's:

  1. Get educated: Take an avalanche safety course to learn about avalanche terrain recognition, snowpack evaluation, route finding, rescue techniques, and safe travel practices in avalanche terrain.
  2. Check avalanche forecasts: Before heading into avalanche terrain, check local avalanche forecasts and weather conditions to assess the risk level and make informed decisions.
  3. Carry essential avalanche gear: Always carry the necessary avalanche safety equipment, including an avalanche transceiver (also known as a beacon), probe, and shovel. Make sure you know how to properly use this equipment.
  4. Travel with partners: Never travel alone in avalanche terrain. Always go with at least one partner, preferably more, and stay within visual and voice contact of each other.
  5. Practice safe travel techniques: Move one at a time through avalanche terrain, minimizing exposure to avalanche hazards. Avoid crossing under or stopping above avalanche paths.
  6. Communicate effectively: Establish clear communication protocols with your partners, including predetermined meeting points, signals, and emergency procedures.
  7. Watch for warning signs: Be vigilant for signs of unstable snow, such as recent avalanches, cracking or collapsing of the snowpack, and visible signs of wind-loading or snowdrifts.
  8. Stay informed about changing conditions: Continuously assess snowpack stability and terrain features as you travel, and be prepared to adjust your plans or turn back if conditions deteriorate.
  9. Know how to self-rescue: Practice self-rescue techniques, including deploying your avalanche transceiver, probing, and digging efficiently. Carry a rescue kit and know how to use it effectively.
  10. Report observations: Share your observations and experiences with local avalanche forecast centers or authorities to contribute to the overall understanding of avalanche conditions in the area.

Don'ts:

  1. Don't ignore avalanche warnings: Take avalanche warnings seriously and avoid traveling in avalanche terrain when the risk level is high or unstable conditions exist.
  2. Don't rely solely on avalanche gear: While essential, avalanche safety equipment is not a guarantee of safety. Proper training, good decision-making, and safe travel practices are equally important.
  3. Don't assume stability: Even if conditions seem safe or stable, always err on the side of caution and treat avalanche terrain with respect. Avalanche hazards can exist even in seemingly benign terrain.
  4. Don't travel blindly: Avoid blindly following tracks or routes without assessing the current snowpack and avalanche conditions. Conditions can change rapidly, and tracks may lead into hazardous terrain.
  5. Don't underestimate the terrain: Respect the complexity of avalanche terrain and recognize that even small slopes or gullies can produce dangerous avalanches under the right conditions.
  6. Don't overcrowd slopes: Avoid traveling in large groups or congregating in one area, as this can increase the load on the snowpack and the risk of triggering an avalanche.
  7. Don't delay in an emergency: If caught in an avalanche, act quickly to deploy your avalanche transceiver, signal for help, and initiate a rescue. Time is of the essence in avalanche rescues.
  8. Don't forget to practice: Regularly practice avalanche rescue drills with your partners to maintain proficiency and ensure a swift and effective response in case of an emergency.
  9. Don't ignore your instincts: Trust your gut feelings and intuition about the safety of the terrain and conditions. If something doesn't feel right, don't hesitate to change your plans or turn back.
  10. Don't neglect to debrief: After each outing in avalanche terrain, take the time to debrief with your partners, discussing what went well, what could be improved, and any lessons learned for future trips.
